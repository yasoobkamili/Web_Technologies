Do You Know Me?

I'm Yasoob Kamili, a curious developer with a screwdriver in one hand and a trekking pole in the other. Whether I’m exploring alpine lakes and hidden meadows or diving deep into the internals of code and hardware, I live for the thrill of discovery. Peaks excite me—both in the mountains and in technology. I like to open almost everything, from devices to ideas, just to see how they work and how I can make them better. Sometimes, though, things don’t work after I open them—but that’s part of the fun. This blog is where I share my climbs, my code, and everything in between.

Can I Walk on Mountains?

I’ve always been drawn to mountains, even as a child. There’s something about their towering presence that captured my imagination. As I grew, that fascination turned into a passion for trekking. So, can I walk on mountains? Yes—and I do, every chance I get. For me, mountains aren’t just landscapes; they’re challenges, teachers, and quiet companions. I’ve wandered through alpine lakes, traced meadows kissed by clouds, and stood beneath peaks that made me feel small in the best way. Trekking is where I find clarity—it’s where my questions echo back with unexpected answers. Every trail I take is a step closer to understanding not just the terrain, but myself.

Tales from The Trails

Dumail, The First Trail

11 July 2021
My first real encounter with the wild began on the trail to Dumail and Marchoi, tucked deep in the Sindh Valley of Kashmir. We started from Naranag, a small temple village that sits at around 2,130 meters (6,988 ft) above sea level. The trail began gently enough, winding through a forest of tall pine and silver birch, with the rhythmic sound of the Wangath River accompanying us. But soon, it grew steeper—each switchback gaining elevation quickly as we moved toward Dumail, which lies roughly 5 km ahead at about 2,700 meters. The path was a mix of muddy patches, rocky outcrops, and occasional snow-laced clearings. At Dumail, where two rivers meet under a canopy of trees, the landscape opened into raw alpine beauty. From there, the route to Marchoi climbed further, challenging my breath and my boots as we approached Marchoi Top, near 3,000 meters elevation, flanked by ridges that wore the last snow of spring like fading memories. It wasn’t just a trek—it was a test of will, lungs, and legs, and the start of a story I’ve been walking ever since.
